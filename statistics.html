The Statistics

The data is clear — disparities are real, measurable, and urgent.

For low-income Black women, the disparities in pregnancy outcomes and healthcare coverage are not abstract — they are measurable, documented, and demand urgent policy action.

3–4x

Black women are 3 to 4 times more likely to die from pregnancy-related causes than white women regardless of income or education level.
(Chinn et al., 2021)

7.5 Years

Black women aged 49–55 are biologically 7.5 years older than their white counterparts — a direct result of chronic stress and poverty that begins during and after pregnancy.
(Chinn et al., 2021)

27%

Chronic stress and poverty account for 27% of the biological aging difference between Black and white women — stress that is compounded during pregnancy.
(Chinn et al., 2021)

$5,500

Black women earn on average $5,500 less per year than the U.S. average for women — making consistent healthcare coverage during pregnancy out of reach for many.
(Chinn et al., 2021)

18.8% vs 5.8%

Example — Iowa: Black residents are uninsured at 18.8% compared to just 5.8% for white residents — more than 3 times the rate, leaving Black women without prenatal coverage. This pattern is repeated across nearly every U.S. state.
(Commonwealth Fund, 2026)
